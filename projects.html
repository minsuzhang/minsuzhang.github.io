My research goal

I desire to advance neural interface technology towards implementing closed-loop neural control. This objective has led me to pursue research in electrical engineering, with focus in the areas of signal processing, biomedical devices, and neuroengineering. To advance this ambition, I am working on transcranial magnetic stimulation for treating memory dysfunctions with Prof. José del R. Millán at The University of Texas at Austin. Prior to this experience, I participated in microelectromechanical system neural probe research at the Korea Institute of Science and Technology and modeled the characteristics of polymer composite materials through complex network analysis at the Korea University.

    • MEMS system for studying long-range neural circuits

    KIST (2020)

    ×

    I have been building a microelectromechanical system for neural circuitry investigation. Our goal was to build an electrophysiology system for verifying intricately structured long-range neural circuits in thick rodent slices. This system could extract single-unit extracellular recordings from multiple distant regions in the brain and optogenetically stimulate cell-type specific neurons. While conducting this research, I learned skills in microsystem design for minimizing the electromagnetic interference coupling and biological experiment techniques such as slice preparation and immunohistochemistry staining.

    • Modeling the resistivity of CNT polymer composites

    Korea University (2019)

    ×

    Intrigued in understanding the emergent behaviors of brain-like complex network structures, I participated in a polymer composite modeling project with Prof. Sang Hyun Lee at the Korea University. The research was aimed at analyzing the conflicting electrical conductivity behavior that occurs when micro-scale and nano-scale secondary particulate fillers are mixed in polymer composites through complex network analysis. Our team simulated the polymer composite structure in MATLAB and measured the electrical conductivity through Monte Carlo simulations. We then verified our hypothesis of the emergent conductivity characteristics by applying Voronoi geometry and percolation theory to the system. In this project, I played a pivotal role in building a 3-D carbon nanotube network simulator and accelerating it by employing discretization techniques and C extension functions. This work was published in June of 2019.

    While conducting this research, I was intrigued by the concept of emergence, in which a phenomenon is unnoticed at the individual level of entities but rises to the surface as the system scales up. I believe that the brain can also be modeled as a complex system of neurons, with various cognitive functions corresponding to the various emergent behaviors. This idea prompted me to research interfaces that can examine neural activities from the circuit to systems level to fully elucidate the underlying brain functions.

    • S.H. Park, J. Hwang, G.S. Park, J.H. Ha, M. Zhang, D. Kim, D.J. Yun, S. Lee, S.H. Lee, Modeling the electrical resistivity of polymer composites with segregated structures, Nature Communications, 2019 (Paper)

    • Implementation of min-max clustering in Zigbee network

    Korea University (2019)

    ×

    Implementing the min-max clustering - an idea introduced in the NIPS 2017 conference paper - using microprocessor Zigbee network was my graduation project topic. Min-max clustering is a belief propagation algorithm that results in forming clusters with the maximum cost of agents minimized. For instance, if we consider the distance between agents as a cost, this algorithm converges to clusters which the longest distance between members within the clusters minimized.

    Our team followed through the mathematical derivations of the message beliefs in the paper by implementing it in Matlab. We then implemented a mesh structure network in the Application Programming Interface mode via Zigbee communication, and enhanced the network to accommodate up to nine agents. In the end, our algorithm converged in 40 iterations for nine agents system and earned us the highest grade in the lecture presentation.

    • Reference: Srinivasa et al., Min-Max Propagation, Proc. of NIPS, 2017

    • Channel capacity of CSK via agent-based modeling

    Korea University (2018)

    ×

    The problem that this research addressed was how to improve the channel capacity of color shift keying (CSK) modulation in the visible light communication system. To resolve the issue, I developed an algorithm that produces the symbol constellations that satisfy the dimming requirements with reduced computational efforts through agent-based modeling. My idea of applying agent-based modeling to solve the CSK problem was inspired by the computational models of the brain. I intended to emulate the neural network, which employs low-complexity computations of nuclei collectively to perform high-level inferences. Therefore, I set each symbol as an agent and solved the constraint satisfaction problem through distributed computations using the software NetLogo.

    As a result, the devised method noticeably relieved computational demands while being free of differential approximation errors incurred in other existing approaches. This work and an improved version of the algorithm, which included signal-dependent noise situations, were published as first-author papers and presented in poster sessions at two conferences in 2018.

    • M. Zhang, H. Kim, S.H. Lee, Measurement of CSK channel capacity using the constraintsatisfaction problem approach, Proceedings of KICS Joint Summer Conference, 2018 (Paper)
    • M. Zhang, S.H. Lee, Agent-based modeling approach of CSK modulation, Proceedings of Joint Conference on Communications and Informatics, 2018 (Poster)
  • Visible light communication, agent-based modeling

    • EyeWire: Connectome mapping project via machine learning

    Princeton University (2015)

    ×

    My research in neuroengineering began during an internship at the Princeton University Neuroscience Institute in 2015. While there, I worked on the connectomics project EyeWire in the computational neuroscience lab. Under the guidance of Prof. Sebastian Seung, our team built a software program that reconstructs 3-D models of neurons from 2-D two-photon microscopy images by leveraging machine learning techniques. This program collected parts of various neuron structures through crowdsourcing and used them to train the deep convolutional network, which then classified the retinal cells. While participating in the project, I significantly improved the data assessment algorithm of the starburst amacrine cell by computing its stratification profile in C++. Once it became operational, our system exceeded expectations; it mapped the structures of over 3,000 neurons and attracted 2.7 million online users.

    This experience taught me the significance of theoretical and computational approaches to the nervous system, as well as the fundamentals of deep learning algorithms. Furthermore, I was able to envision the advances in neuroscience research that would be possible when vast field of view recording technology for individual neural activities would be developed and available. Such ideas led me to constantly contemplate on the mechanisms of the brain and cognition from an engineering perspective.

    • Reference: Bae et al., Digital Museum of Retinal Ganglion Cells with Dense Anatomy and Physiology, Cell, 2018
